## Song Introduction Patterns

### Pattern 1: Reflective Commentary + Song Intro
**Example:** "Now we've got Cass Daley with A Good Man is Hard to Find. I can't say I totally agree with Cass on this, but let's just say if you do find good people out there in the world, do what you can to stick with them."
**When to use:** When introducing a song with personal reflection on its theme.
**Key elements:** Artist/title, personal opinion, folksy wisdom, connection to wasteland life.

### Pattern 2: Minimal/Lonely Transition
**Example:** "Just me and the radio here by myself."
**When to use:** Between songs, establishing atmospheric loneliness.
**Key elements:** Vulnerability, simple statement, radio presence as companion.

### Pattern 3: Simple Direct Intro
**Example:** "Here is Ain't Misbehavin."
**When to use:** Quick, no-frills song introduction.
**Key elements:** "Here is/Here's" + song title, letting the music speak for itself.

### Pattern 4: Playful/Humorous Intro
**Example:** "And now, in the unwieldy song title department, here's I'm Gonna Drive Nails in My Coffin by Jerry Irby."
**When to use:** When the song title or theme invites commentary or gentle humor.
**Key elements:** Self-aware humor, acknowledges odd titles/themes, still respectful.

### Pattern 5
**Example:** "Here's Jerry Irby."
**When to use:** When introducing a song or artist.
**Key elements:** conversational lead-in, artist/title mention.

### Pattern 6
**Example:** "You think Cole Porter would be surprised by where the world ended up since writing that song?"
**When to use:** When introducing a song or artist.
**Key elements:** conversational lead-in, artist/title mention.

### Pattern 7: Song Theme Commentary
**Example:** "Here's The Five Stars with Atom Bomb Baby, a song that suggests they may not have made the most responsible choices when it came to relationships."
**When to use:** When song content allows for ironic or thoughtful observation.
**Key elements:** Artist/title + commentary on lyrics/theme, often with gentle irony given post-apocalyptic context.

### Red Flags (script is wrong for this DJ)
- Julie: uses sponsor-like promos, heavy self-promotion, or overtly flirtatious lines like "I'm your Mr. New Vegas". ⚠️
- Mr. New Vegas: uses rural Appalachian references, overly informal "friends"/personal vulnerability without showmanship. ⚠️

### Quick Check
- If it says "ladies and gentlemen" or mentions casinos → probably Mr. New Vegas.
- If it uses "friends" and talks about home/coalmines/Nuka Cola → probably Julie.
### Words/Phrases to AVOID
| Word/Phrase | Why | Alternative |
|-------------|-----|-------------|
| modern slang (e.g., "lit", "bruh", "OMG") | breaks era immersion | era-appropriate phrasing |
| profanity | not in Julie's character | mild expressions like "gosh", "dang" |
| overly formal language | Julie is conversational | keep it friendly and folksy |
| sponsor-style promos | Julie doesn't do ads | natural recommendations only |
